Dents: paintless when we can, refinished when we can't

Paintless dent repair (PDR) works the metal back to shape from the back side of the panel with specialized rods and picks, so the original factory finish is never touched. No filler, no primer, no repaint — which means faster turnaround, a lower bill, and no color-match risk. It's the ideal fix for the everyday stuff:

  • Parking-lot door dings and shopping-cart dents
  • Hail damage — dozens of dents in a single session
  • Minor creases where the paint hasn't cracked or chipped
  • Small dents on aluminum hoods, doors and quarter panels
  • Body-line dents that need shape restored, not filled

Not every dent is a PDR candidate. If the paint is cracked, the metal is stretched, the dent sits on a hard body edge, or there's rust, we switch to conventional repair and refinish — working the metal, filling and sanding, then color-matching and blending fresh clearcoat so the panel disappears into the rest of the car.

Is my dent a PDR candidate?

The honest answer is that we look at it in person, but two things decide it: is the paint intact, and how far is the dent from an edge. Round dents in the middle of a flat panel with unbroken paint are textbook PDR. Sharp creases, cracked paint, and dents on the very lip of a panel usually need refinishing.

Frame & unibody straightening

A bent frame is a structural problem, not a cosmetic one. The unibody is what holds your suspension geometry, protects the cabin, and gives crumple zones something to work against. If it's off — even by a few millimeters — the car can pull, eat tires, and behave unpredictably in the next impact. We don't guess at it.

Every frame job starts on a computerized measuring system that compares your car against the manufacturer's original three-dimensional specs. We anchor the vehicle and apply controlled hydraulic pulls to bring the structure back to factory datum, re-measuring as we go until it's within roughly 1mm of spec.

  • Full computerized measure before, during and after the pull
  • Multi-point hydraulic anchoring and controlled straightening
  • Structural sections repaired to OEM procedures
  • Steel and aluminum handled with the right process for each

Steel vs. aluminum

The two behave nothing alike. Steel can be heated and pulled and generally forgives more. Aluminum is stiffer, work-hardens, and can't take the same heat — it often needs dedicated tooling, a separate clean work area to avoid cross-contamination, and manufacturer-specified rivet-bonding instead of welding. We use the correct method for your vehicle's construction, not a one-size-fits-all pull.

How a dent & frame job runs

1 · Inspect & measure

We assess each dent for PDR versus refinish, and if there's any suspicion of structural damage we put the car on the measuring bench to see exactly where it deviates from factory spec.

2 · Paintless dent repair

Where the panel qualifies, we work the metal back from behind with rods and picks — no filler, no paint — until the surface reads flat and the reflections run clean.

3 · Frame & unibody pull

Structural cars are anchored and pulled hydraulically back to datum, re-measured continuously, with steel or aluminum handled by the manufacturer's process.

4 · Conventional repair & refinish

Dents that can't go paintless are worked, filled and sanded, then color-matched with a spectrophotometer and blended in a downdraft booth for a factory-grade finish.

5 · Suspension & alignment check

After any frame pull we check the suspension and set the wheel alignment — a straightened frame is only truly fixed when the car tracks straight and wears its tires evenly again.

6 · Road test & QC

A final inspection and road test confirm the car sits right, tracks true and shows no trace of the damage before we hand back the keys.

Dent & frame repair questions

What's paintless dent repair and is my dent a candidate?

PDR reshapes metal from behind the panel with special tools, leaving your factory paint untouched — no filler or repaint. It's ideal when the paint is intact and the dent sits away from a hard edge, like door dings, hail and minor creases. Cracked paint, stretched metal or edge dents usually need conventional repair instead.

Can a bent frame really be fixed safely?

Yes, when it's done on a computerized measuring bench. We anchor the car and apply controlled hydraulic pulls back to the manufacturer's original dimensions, re-measuring until it's within roughly 1mm of factory datum. If a section is too compromised to restore safely, we tell you — we won't sign off on structure we don't trust.

Will hail damage be covered by insurance?

Hail is typically covered under the comprehensive part of your policy, often with no impact to your rates since it's a no-fault event. Many hail cars are a perfect fit for PDR, which keeps costs down. We document the damage and work directly with your carrier on the claim.

Do you check alignment after frame work?

Always. A frame pull moves the mounting points that set your suspension geometry, so we check the suspension and set the wheel alignment afterward. Skipping it leaves the car pulling to one side and chewing through tires even when the frame itself is back to spec.
