Auto glass done right

On a modern car the windshield is a structural and sensor-mounting part, not a simple pane. It helps the roof resist a rollover, it positions the forward camera, and it has to seal perfectly against water and wind noise. A rushed glass job with the wrong part or thin adhesive can leak, whistle, or throw the camera's aim off by degrees.

Here's what a proper glass replacement includes:

  • OEM or OEM-equivalent windshields, door and quarter glass
  • Fresh factory-grade urethane with a safe drive-away cure time
  • New moldings, clips and seals — never reused, brittle rubber
  • Correct glass for cameras: the right bracket, tint band and acoustic layer
  • Rain-sensor, heated-element and antenna transfers reconnected
  • Water-leak and wind-noise check before the car goes back to you

Why aftermarket glass can break calibration

The forward camera reads the road through a small window in the glass. Cheap aftermarket windshields often have slightly different optical clarity, curvature or bracket placement — enough that the camera can't be calibrated at all, or calibrates to the wrong reference. We match the glass to what the sensor expects, so the recalibration actually holds.

What ADAS calibration is

Modern cars carry a suite of ADAS — Advanced Driver-Assistance Systems. A forward-facing camera and radar behind the windshield and grille feed automatic emergency braking and adaptive cruise control; corner and bumper radars run blind-spot monitoring and rear cross-traffic alert; the same camera reads lane lines for lane-keep assist. These sensors are aimed to fractions of a degree.

Any time that geometry moves, the aim has to be reset. Recalibration is required after:

  • A windshield replacement — the camera bracket is disturbed
  • Any collision that touches a bumper, fender or the front end
  • Sensor, grille or bumper cover replacement
  • Suspension or wheel-alignment work that changes ride height

Static vs. dynamic — and why it matters

Static calibration is done in the shop: we park the car dead-level, set manufacturer targets at exact measured distances, and let the camera re-learn its reference. Dynamic calibration is a controlled road drive at set speeds so the system re-learns against real lane lines and traffic. Many vehicles need both. Skip it and the car may brake late, drift, or fail to warn — while looking perfectly fine on the dash.

Our glass & calibration process

1 · Identify & scan

We pull your VIN's exact glass and sensor build, then run a pre-repair scan to log every fault code before we start — so nothing gets blamed on us that was already there.

2 · Remove & prep

Old glass out, the pinch-weld cleaned and primed, camera bracket protected. Any corrosion is addressed before new adhesive touches the frame.

3 · OEM glass & urethane

The correct windshield or glass is set in fresh factory-grade urethane, moldings and clips renewed, and the car held for the adhesive's safe drive-away cure time.

4 · Static calibration

On a level floor with manufacturer targets at measured distances, the forward camera and radar are recalibrated to spec — the step aftermarket glass often can't pass.

5 · Dynamic calibration

Where the vehicle requires it, a technician road-tests at the specified speeds so the system re-learns against live lane markings and traffic.

6 · Post-scan & insurance

A post-repair scan confirms zero calibration faults, and we handle the glass and calibration claim with all major carriers directly.

Glass & ADAS questions

Why does a windshield replacement need a calibration now?

Because the forward-facing camera that runs automatic braking and lane-keep is mounted to the windshield. Removing and re-setting the glass moves that camera, so it has to be recalibrated to spec or its aim can be off by degrees.

What is ADAS calibration?

ADAS stands for Advanced Driver-Assistance Systems — the cameras and radar behind your glass and bumpers. Calibration precisely re-aims those sensors, either statically with targets in the shop, dynamically on a road drive, or both, depending on your vehicle.

Will my insurance cover glass and calibration?

In most cases yes — carriers treat calibration as a required part of the glass or collision repair. We bill the glass and the calibration together and work directly with all major insurers on the paperwork.

How long does it take?

A glass swap alone is usually same-day, but the urethane needs a safe cure time and calibration adds a few hours — dynamic calibration also needs a road drive. Most glass-plus-ADAS jobs are done in a day; we'll give you the exact timeline up front.
