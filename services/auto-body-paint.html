What our paint work covers

Modern factory paint is a layered system — primer, tinted basecoat, and a clear protective topcoat — not a single bucket of color. Cars also fade as they age, so the paint on your five-year-old fender is no longer the shade that left the factory. Spray it with the "book" formula and it will read as a mismatched patch in daylight.

We handle the full refinishing job:

  • Spectrophotometer scan of your actual, aged paint
  • Custom formula mixing and sprayout test cards before the car
  • Factory-grade basecoat plus two-stage clearcoat
  • Blending into adjacent panels so no edge is visible
  • Color-sanding and machine cut-and-polish to a mirror finish
  • Optional ceramic coating for long-term gloss and protection

Why cheap respray jobs fail

A quick single-stage "all-in-one" respray skips the clearcoat and the blend. It looks passable in the shop's shade for a season, then the color oxidizes, chalks, and mismatches the rest of the car within a year or two. We refinish to the manufacturer's layered spec so the panel ages at the same rate as the metal around it.

Our refinishing process

1 · Prep & color read

The panel is stripped, straightened, and sanded to a feather edge, then primed and blocked flat. We scan the surrounding paint with a spectrophotometer and mix a formula matched to your car's current color, not the factory book value.

2 · Mask & isolate

Glass, trim, badges, and every gap are masked and back-taped so overspray never touches an edge you didn't intend to refinish. Panels that will be blended into are cleaned and scuffed for adhesion.

3 · Spray in the downdraft booth

Basecoat is laid down in a sealed, filtered downdraft booth where air pulls dust and solvent straight to the floor. We blend the color out into adjacent panels, then flow two coats of clearcoat over the whole blend zone.

4 · Bake & cure

The booth ramps to a controlled bake so the clearcoat cross-links to a hard, chip-resistant cure instead of staying soft for weeks the way an air-dried respray does.

5 · Cut & polish

Once cured, we color-sand out any texture and machine-polish the clear to a factory gloss, so the refinished area matches the depth and shine of the original paint — no dull patch, no orange-peel mismatch.

6 · QC & ceramic option

We inspect the blend under corrected and natural light from multiple angles. If you've added a ceramic coating, it's applied last over the fully cured clear for long-term gloss and easier washing.

Auto body paint questions

Will the new paint match my existing color?

Yes. We scan your car's actual, aged paint with a spectrophotometer and mix the formula to that reading — then blend into the neighboring panels so the eye can't find a transition line, even in direct sun.

How long does paint work take?

A single-panel refinish is usually 2–4 business days including prep, spray, bake, and cut-and-polish. Multi-panel or full-vehicle jobs run longer; you'll get a written timeline at drop-off.

Is there a warranty on the paint?

Our refinishing carries a lifetime warranty against peeling, cracking, and delamination for as long as you own the vehicle — because it's sprayed to the manufacturer's layered basecoat-and-clearcoat spec, not a one-coat shortcut.

Can you fix clearcoat peeling or oxidation?

Yes. Failing clear and chalky, oxidized paint can't be polished back once the topcoat is gone — the panel has to be sanded down and refinished. We strip the failed clear, re-shoot basecoat and clearcoat, and blend it into the good paint around it.
